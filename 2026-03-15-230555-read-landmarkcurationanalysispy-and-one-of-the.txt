  Why the Z difference is non-integer here specifically:

  The prone Z being exactly identical is the key clue. Most likely workflow:
  both registrars placed the prone landmark first, and the prone coordinate was
  either copied or agreed upon. For the supine placement, they each
  independently located the cyst — and they landed at X/Y positions 0.5mm apart,
   Y 0.24mm apart, and Z 0.22mm apart.

  The critical insight is that all three coordinates vary continuously here (X,
  Y, and Z all have similar-magnitude sub-mm differences), unlike every other
  point where Z varies in discrete 0.9mm steps while X/Y vary continuously.

  This strongly suggests that for this one pick, at least one registrar was not 
  scrolling through axial slices in the standard 2D view. Instead they were
  likely in:
  - A 3D volume rendering — cursor clicks project a ray into the volume and land
   at whatever depth the renderer picks, giving a fully continuous 3D coordinate
  - A coronal or sagittal reformatted view — in those orientations Z is an
  in-plane axis, so cursor position determines Z continuously just like X/Y in
  axial view
  - A multi-planar reconstruction (MPR) panel that shows all three planes
  simultaneously

  In the standard axial 2D scroll view, Z is locked to whichever slice you
  scrolled to — you can only get Z values at exactly 0, ±0.9, ±1.8 mm, etc. from
   each other. The 0.219 mm difference proves neither registrar was constrained
  to axial slice positions for this pick.

  Is this actually a problem? No — 0.219 mm disagreement in Z is smaller than
  one slice (~0.9 mm) and smaller than the in-plane pixel spacing. The two
  registrars are in excellent agreement on this cyst (total 3D distance ≈ 0.62
  mm). It's just that the picking mode used gave a continuous coordinate rather
  than a discretised one, so it stands out visually in the Bland-Altman as not
  fitting the banding pattern.
